Ryssa banked the hearth the way her grandmother had taught her: slowly, with the iron
hook, coaxing the coals into a single low red eye that would keep through the night.
She had done it so many times her hands no longer asked her to pay attention.

The knock came at the hour the hearth went quiet.

She knew who it would be before she opened the door. You always knew. The Envoy had
passed through her mother's house once, and her great-aunt's, and — if the stories
were to be trusted — her great-grandfather's. They never came for the same house
twice, and they never came for anyone who expected them.

The Envoy stood on her step with snow on their pale shoulders, though there had been
no snow that winter. They bowed, small and courtly, and offered the letter with both
hands.

"For you," they said. Their voice was a soft, ruined thing. "From one who knew your
line."

She took it. The wax seal was the color of an old bruise. The hand on the front of
the envelope was unmistakably her grandmother's — the same pinched loop on the *y*,
the same pause between *Rys* and *sa* as if the writer had always been unsure where
her name should break.

When Ryssa looked up to ask how, the Envoy was no longer on the step. Only the snow
remained, a small precise drift of it where they had been standing, already beginning
to melt.

She closed the door. She went to the hearth, where the single red eye was still
watching, and sat with the letter in her lap for a long time before she broke the
seal.

What she read, she never spoke of. But the next morning she lit a second candle
beside the first, and set it on the window-ledge where nothing had ever burned
before, and she kept that candle lit for the rest of her life.

When visitors asked, she only said: *"For the one who wrote."*
