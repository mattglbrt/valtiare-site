The Orchard was not quite silent. It never was. The glass trees held the day's faint
warmth and let it out in small, slow chimes — a sound like someone setting down a cup
they did not want to set down.

Saelvyn walked the familiar path. Past the bent tree that the pilgrims called the
Widow. Past the hollow where a lantern had been left burning, not yet guttered. Past
the three small stones a child had stacked that morning, which Saelvyn bent and
straightened without thinking.

At the Keeper, they stopped.

They had been coming here for a long time.

They knelt, which was not easy anymore, and drew from the inside of their cloak a
single taper — unlit, wax the color of bone. They set it at the base of the Keeper's
trunk, where the soil had gone soft with offerings. Then they removed the brass
half-mask, and set it beside the taper, and spoke.

> "It is me. It is still me. I know you can hear, because you remember everything,
> and I have told you everything I am. I am here to say the name I have never said
> out loud. I want it out of me. I want it left here. I want the trees to keep it
> and not give it back, and if you must tell it back to someone, tell it back to
> the wind."

They said the name.

It was a small sound, and the Orchard held it.

Then Saelvyn put the mask back on and stood, slower than before, and walked away
between the glass trees, which chimed softly as they passed — softer than they had
chimed in four hundred years, as if something heavy had finally been set down.
