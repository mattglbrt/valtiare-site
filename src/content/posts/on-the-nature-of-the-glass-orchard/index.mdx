There are, in the libraries of Vaelloss, at least three respectable accounts of the
Glass Orchard's origin. None of them agree. None of them are entirely trusted. And
there is a fourth account, older and untranscribed, which the people who live nearest
the Orchard hold quietly, and repeat only to children.

## The Cinder-Seed Account

The oldest written account, attributed to the scholar Vessara of Low Emberwatch,
claims the Orchard grew from seeds of cooled starlight that fell during the last
great dimming. She writes of farmers finding "small, warm stones, no heavier than a
grain of wheat," and planting them out of curiosity. The trees, she says, grew slowly
— one handspan a century — until they became what they are now: tall, translucent,
holding inside them the embers of stars that had burned out before any person alive
was born.

## The Single-Organism Account

Three centuries later, Hallen of the Shale Academy dismissed Vessara's account as
romantic folklore and proposed instead that the entire Orchard is a single organism —
"a vast and patient creature," in his words, "wearing the shape of many trees for
reasons of its own." He points out that every tree in the Orchard shares the same
faint internal light; that cutting any branch causes the entire grove to dim for a
moment; that the trees grow, he claims, in patterns too deliberate to be natural.

Hallen never published the final volume of his study. He was last seen walking into
the Orchard on a winter afternoon, notebook in hand, and was never recovered.

## The Furnace Account

The most recent account, and the one taught in the academies, is the Furnace Account
of Adelen Vos. She argues that the Orchard is a residue — the physical cooling remains
of an ancient planetary furnace, the kind the old builders used to warm whole regions
at once. When the furnace failed, its escaping heat crystallized as it rose through
the soil, becoming, over centuries, the glass trees we see now. The embers inside
are, she says, simply the last trapped warmth, still slowly leaving.

This account is tidy. It is also the only one that has ever been successfully used to
predict anything about the Orchard's behavior.

## The Fourth Account

The people who live in the settlements around the Orchard do not believe any of these
stories. They will tell you, if you ask gently, that the Orchard is a **promise**.

They cannot always say what the promise was, or to whom, or by whom. But they say
that on the night the last great star died, someone stood in this place and made a
vow that certain things would not be forgotten — and the trees grew up afterward to
hold that vow.

The pilgrims who walk the Orchard at dusk are not there for scholarship. They are
there to add something small to what is already being kept. A name. A memory. A
prayer they could not say anywhere else.

Whichever account is true — and perhaps they all are, in the way old things often
are — the Orchard continues to hold what it has been given. It glows, faintly, in a
world that is otherwise cooling, and asks nothing of anyone who visits except that
they walk quietly, and listen.
