The Envoy arrives on foot, always alone, always in the hour before a hearth is
due to be banked for the night. They carry a satchel of bone-white leather with
letters inside — each sealed with wax the color of old bruises, each addressed to
someone who died generations ago.

The letters are always delivered. Recipients are found — descendants, namesakes,
strangers who happen to share a face. When the letter is handed over, the Envoy bows
once, steps back into shadow, and is gone.

No one has yet read one of these letters aloud. Those who have opened them in private
have refused afterward to speak of their contents, except to say that the hand was
familiar, and the grief was new.
