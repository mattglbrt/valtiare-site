The Fractured Isles are what remains of a country.

For most of its long history, the land was called **Manira** — a northern
country of deep stone and patient cities. Its people lived in keeps built
into hillsides, traded with Ruhar to the south and Emuin to the north, and
kept their rituals around the shimmering caverns of the Svirfneblin
strongholds beneath them. They were known as builders and keepers, a
people who measured wealth in how long a thing could be made to last.

Then the earth fractured.

The Fracturing tore Manira apart. The ground itself rose into the sky in
great jagged shards, stone ripping free of stone. What should have fallen
did not fall. Instead the broken pieces of the country drifted upward and
outward, and then held there, suspended — bound together by thin filaments
of warm light no one has yet been able to name or measure.

Travelers began to call the place the Fractured Isles. The name stuck.

Today, villages cling to the edges of stone rafts that used to be hills.
Bridges are ropes and planks thrown across gaps that were once unbroken
meadow. The Svirfneblin of Kraaz'Duun still live in their caverns, though
in places their ceiling is now the open sky. No one agrees on what caused
the Fracturing. The light that holds the isles together has not been
explained. The people who still live there have, for the most part,
stopped asking.
