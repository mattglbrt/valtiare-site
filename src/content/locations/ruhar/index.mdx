Ruhar shares its northern border with what used to be Manira. When Manira
fractured — when its earth tore open and its stone rose into the sky in
shards that would not fall — Ruhar answered in a way no one expected, and
no one has yet explained. The land began to grow.

What had been a mixed country of plains, low forests, riverlands, and small
cities became, over the course of a single long year, a single vast and
tangled wilderness. The old roads were swallowed by creeper and root. The
cities were reclaimed down to their foundations. The rivers thickened with
reeds and new, unfamiliar fish. The beasts grew stranger. The trees grew
taller. And everything grew *faster*, as if Ruhar were making up for some
long patience no one had known it was keeping.

Travelers speak of oaks with faces worn into the bark, of streams that
change direction after nightfall, and of settlements so deep within the
canopy that the outside world has become a rumor to them. The Svirfneblin
keep old halls beneath its roots. Pilgrims and exiles walk its paths. The
wounded are carried here more often than the wounded ever expect to be
carried anywhere — the forest has a way of finding them.
