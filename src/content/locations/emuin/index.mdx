Emuin shares its southern border with what used to be Manira. When Manira
fractured and rose into the sky, and when Ruhar to the further south
answered by growing, Emuin answered too — in a way no one wanted, and no
one has yet been able to undo.

The land began to thin. Fields that had yielded grain for centuries turned
pale and dry. Streams went slow, then shallow, then still. The oaks and
pines of Emuin's northern ridges kept their shape but lost their weight;
travelers say that if you push a trunk now, the bark gives like an old
bruise and the wood beneath is full of dust.

The beasts grew gaunt. The people grew quiet. Children in Emuin are born
smaller than they should be and older than they should be, with eyes that
look past the person speaking to them. Candles flicker more often here.
Food does not satisfy the way it used to. Songs are forgotten between the
third verse and the fourth.

Some in Emuin believe the Fracturing took something from their country and
gave it to Ruhar — that the green which bloomed so violently in the south
was the missing weight of the north. No one knows if this is true. The
Emuinese do not argue about it. Arguing takes strength they cannot spare.
